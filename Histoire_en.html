Indigenous Peoples

The Grand Canyon has been inhabited for over 10,000 years. Several indigenous peoples have developed their cultures, spiritual traditions, and lifestyles adapted to this unique environment.

The Ancestral Puebloans

Villages, agriculture, pottery, cliff dwellings.

The Hopis

One of the oldest peoples, traditions and myths linked to the canyon.

The Havasupai

Settled in the valleys, renowned for their crops and gardens.

The Navajo

Arrived later, important spiritual territory.

The arrival of Europeans

1540: Cárdenas expedition

Sent by Spain, Garcia López de Cárdenas reaches the south rim of the canyon. His group discovers the immensity of the site but fails to descend. He is the first known European to have observed it.

19th century: explorations and surveys

In the 19th century, soldiers, explorers, and scientists crossed the region. They made the first surveys of the canyon, despite the dangers and lack of paths. The site began to be mapped with precision.

1869: John Wesley Powell expedition

In 1869, John Wesley Powell led a descent of the Colorado River with nine men. They navigated unknown rapids and collected essential data. This expedition became the first scientific exploration of the canyon.

A protected site

Recognized for its cultural and natural importance, the Grand Canyon became a National Monument in 1908 and then a National Park in 1919. Today, it is listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

The Grand Canyon, recognized for its exceptional universal value, represents a major natural and cultural heritage. Its history, landscapes, and the traditions of indigenous peoples make it an irreplaceable site. UNESCO works to preserve this unique place for future generations.

"Carved out by the Colorado River, the Grand Canyon nearly 1,500 m deep is the most spectacular gorge in the world. Located in the state of Arizona, it cuts across the Grand Canyon National Park."
